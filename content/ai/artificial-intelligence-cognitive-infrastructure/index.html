Executive Summary

Artificial Intelligence is one of the principal forces shaping the global future toward 2035 because it has moved beyond software automation, search, chatbots and experimental digital tools. It is becoming a general-purpose cognitive capability embedded across knowledge work, education, public administration, science, media, energy systems, national security, enterprise productivity and international competition.

The central argument of this report is that AI is no longer only a digital tool. It is becoming cognitive infrastructure: an embedded layer through which societies search, write, reason, code, learn, translate, design, decide, govern, communicate and organize knowledge.

AI is a future-shaping force because it is already reorganizing work, knowledge, power, infrastructure, governance and global development.

1. Core Thesis

Artificial Intelligence qualifies as a global future-shaping force because it changes the method by which societies process information, produce knowledge, automate tasks and scale decision-making. Earlier general-purpose technologies mechanized physical labour, accelerated communication, digitized information or connected people through networks. AI extends this lineage but differs in kind: contemporary AI systems interpret information, generate knowledge-like outputs, classify patterns, produce content, assist reasoning, support coding and increasingly coordinate multi-step tasks through agentic systems.

This report organizes AI’s future-shaping effect around three interlinked pathways: cognitive transformation, economic transformation and institutional transformation.

Cognitive transformationHow societies think

AI changes how people learn, write, analyse, design, research and make decisions.

Economic transformationHow value is created

AI changes productivity, business models, labour demand and enterprise competition.

Institutional transformationHow systems govern

AI changes regulation, public services, education, security and infrastructure planning.

2. Literature Review: AI as Global Cognitive Infrastructure

The phrase “AI as cognitive infrastructure” should not be treated as a slogan. It is an emerging conceptual framework found in academic, policy, economic and critical literature. It describes a transition from AI as a discrete tool toward AI as an embedded system that structures how people perceive information, assess relevance, produce knowledge and make decisions.

Conceptual origins

The term “cognitive infrastructure” predates the current generative-AI wave. Braden Allenby introduced the idea as a way of describing institutions, technologies, services and products that provide the functional elements of cognition — from perception and intelligibility to problem-solving. This early framing included sensors, connected devices, facial-recognition systems, autonomous vehicles, social-media platforms and AI systems that process vast streams of human and machine-generated information.

System 0 and Cognitive Infrastructure Studies

Giuseppe Riva’s recent work proposes Cognitive Infrastructure Studies built around the concept of System 0: an invisible, non-human layer of distributed cognition that precedes fast intuitive reasoning and slow analytical reasoning. Search engines, recommender systems, ranking algorithms, language models and conversational agents filter, pre-select and frame information before it reaches conscious processing.

Three mechanisms are especially important: anticipatory personalization, adaptive invisibility and automation of relevance judgment. These mechanisms shift part of the locus of epistemic agency away from individual human attention and toward machine-mediated systems.

What the literature supports

The literature supports the claim that AI increasingly meets the formal definition of infrastructure: embeddedness, invisibility-in-use, dependency, standard-setting authority and systemic reach. It does not yet establish as settled empirical fact that AI use degrades human reasoning at population scale. That remains a research hypothesis requiring evidence, governance and public awareness.

3. Scientific and Technical Foundations

The technical foundation of the current AI wave rests on machine learning, deep learning, large-scale data pipelines, foundation models, large language models, multimodal systems, diffusion models, reinforcement learning, high-performance computing, cloud infrastructure, specialized chips and increasingly agentic AI systems.

AI systems are not human intelligence. They do not possess consciousness, moral responsibility, lived experience, human wisdom, social accountability or ethical judgment. They perform large classes of information-processing tasks at high speed, large scale and relatively low marginal cost. This is the basis of AI’s power as a general-purpose technology: it industrializes parts of cognition without replicating human cognition as a whole.

Hallucination as a structural limitation

The most consequential limitation is hallucination: the tendency of generative AI systems to produce fluent, confident and plausible-sounding output that is factually incorrect, unsupported or fabricated. In high-stakes domains such as healthcare, law, finance, public administration, education and policy analysis, AI must remain subject to human oversight, source verification, audit, testing and domain-expert review.

5. Sectoral Impact Analysis

AI’s future-shaping power lies in horizontal diffusion. It affects many systems at once rather than only one sector.

Knowledge work and professional services

AI assists with reading, writing, coding, translation, summarization, legal drafting, financial analysis, research support, report generation, customer communication and decision preparation. Many roles are likely to be restructured rather than fully eliminated.

Education and skills

AI can support tutoring, feedback, translation, lesson planning, content generation, personalized learning and administrative support. It also requires new policies for academic integrity, AI literacy, source verification, ethical use and critical thinking.

Business and enterprise transformation

The key transformation is workflow redesign. Organizations that merely add AI chatbots to old processes may see limited gains. Organizations that redesign workflows, data systems, decision processes and accountability structures around AI may achieve deeper value.

Public administration, media and science

AI can improve public services, accelerate research and support analysis, but it also introduces risks of bias, misinformation, surveillance, deepfakes, hallucination and weakened public trust.

6. Governance, Ethics and Institutional Response

AI becomes a recognized future-shaping force when institutions reorganize around it. This has already occurred through OECD principles, UNESCO ethics standards, the European Union AI Act, national AI strategies, AI safety institutes and emerging risk-management systems.

The OECD AI Principles emphasize inclusive growth, human rights, transparency, robustness, safety and accountability. UNESCO’s Recommendation on the Ethics of AI frames AI as a human-rights, ethics and governance issue. The EU AI Act establishes a risk-based framework with obligations for high-risk systems and general-purpose AI models.

Governance is advancing, but it is still catching up with deployment speed. Open tensions include data protection versus bias prevention, agentic AI accountability, fragmented global regulation and uneven institutional capacity.

7. Geopolitical Dimension: Whose Global Cognitive Infrastructure?

If AI is infrastructure, control over it becomes a hard-power question. The global cognitive infrastructure is not being built as a neutral commons. It is being shaped by states, corporations, alliances, supply chains, export controls, chips, data centres, cloud platforms and geopolitical competition.

For many developing countries, full AI sovereignty may be unrealistic in the near term. The practical goal may be AI resilience: the ability to use AI reliably under domestic legal, ethical, educational and operational frameworks while managing dependence on foreign platforms.

8. Projection Toward 2035

By 2035, AI is likely to shape the future through AI-native workflows, labour-market transition, education reinvention, physical-infrastructure competition, governance maturation, trust systems, scientific acceleration and the AI divide.

These pathways are directional expectations based on current evidence, not guaranteed forecasts. They should guide planning, education, governance and strategic foresight.

9. FutureWorld Intelligence Assessment

Artificial Intelligence is one of the five forces shaping the future because it has crossed three thresholds: capability, adoption and institutional response. It can generate, analyse, code, translate, summarize, classify, design, plan and support decisions across many domains. It is already being used across organizations, professions, education, software, media, public systems and everyday life. Governments, international organizations, companies, universities and civil society are creating rules, standards, strategies, investments and governance systems around AI.

The winners of the AI era will not be those who use AI blindly. They will be those who combine AI capability with human judgment, ethical governance, inclusive education, resilient infrastructure, trustworthy institutions, cybersecurity and strategic foresight.

Final Report Statement

Artificial Intelligence is shaping the future because it is already reorganizing knowledge, work, productivity, education, governance, infrastructure, science, security and global competition. It is no longer only a digital tool. It is becoming cognitive infrastructure: an embedded layer through which people, organizations and governments increasingly think, decide, create, learn, communicate and act.

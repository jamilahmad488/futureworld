#009 Climate Justice and Just Transition

Climate action must reduce emissions and risk without transferring costs to vulnerable people, workers, communities, ecosystems or countries with limited capacity to respond.

Strategic definition

Climate justice is the principle that climate risks, responsibilities, benefits and burdens must be assessed through equity, vulnerability, rights, capacity and historical contribution. It asks who is affected, who contributed most to the problem, who has the least capacity to respond and who receives the benefits of climate action.

Just transition is the implementation pathway that makes climate action socially and economically fair. It requires transition planning to address workforce, livelihood, social protection, local economic diversification, gender, community participation and institutional-capacity dimensions.

Core rule

Climate solutions should protect vulnerable communities, respect affected groups, avoid unfair burdens and distribute transition benefits transparently.

A climate intervention is not complete when it reduces emissions or risk. It must also show how people, livelihoods, rights, ecosystems and institutions are protected during the transition.

1Why this principle matters

Climate impacts and climate policies do not affect all groups equally. Poor households, women, youth, Indigenous peoples, informal workers, rural communities, mountain settlements, climate-exposed farmers and people living in hazard-prone areas often face higher risk and lower adaptive capacity. At the same time, some climate policies can create social and economic disruption if they are implemented without consultation, compensation, reskilling, livelihood alternatives or local ownership.

Who is vulnerable?Groups with limited income, access, rights, services or representation.

2Just transition operating framework

Decent work and quality jobsTransition planning should protect workers, create quality employment and support skills development where sectors change.
Social protectionHouseholds and workers affected by transition or climate impacts may require safety nets, compensation, insurance or livelihood support.
Social dialogueWorkers, employers, communities, government and civil society should participate in transition planning.
Gender and inclusionWomen, youth, persons with disabilities and marginalized groups should be included in benefits, decisions and safeguards.
Local economic diversificationRegions dependent on high-risk, high-carbon or climate-sensitive sectors need alternative livelihoods and investment pathways.
Rights and safeguardsClimate action should protect land, resource access, cultural values, Indigenous peoples and community grievance rights.
Adaptation justiceCommunities already facing climate impacts require finance, capacity and protection before losses become irreversible.
International cooperationFinance, technology transfer and capacity-building are essential for equitable transition in developing countries.

4Application to climate finance

Climate finance should not only count tonnes of emissions avoided or hectares restored. It should also demonstrate who benefits, how risks are distributed, whether gender and social inclusion are addressed, and whether vulnerable groups can access the intervention.

For adaptation finance, justice requires prioritizing communities with high exposure and low capacity. For mitigation finance, justice requires protecting workers and communities affected by low-carbon transition, especially where livelihoods depend on land, forests, fuel, agriculture, transport, mining or energy systems.

5Application to field programmes

In restoration, watershed protection, clean energy, disaster-risk reduction and community resilience, just transition requires practical mechanisms.

  • Transparent beneficiary selection
  • Local consultation before project design
  • Women and youth participation
  • Protection of land and resource access
  • Livelihood alternatives where restrictions are introduced
  • Community grievance and feedback systems

7Professional editorial standard

Do not generalize vulnerabilityIdentify which groups are vulnerable, why they are vulnerable and how the intervention reduces that vulnerability.
Do not claim inclusion without proofDocument consultation, participation, grievance systems and benefit-sharing mechanisms.
Do not separate transition from livelihoodsExplain how jobs, skills, income and local economies are protected during change.
Do not ignore gender and social normsAssess whether women and marginalized groups can access benefits and participate in decisions.
Do not report only activity countsMeasure whether justice, resilience and transition outcomes are actually achieved.

Final takeaway

Climate justice and just transition convert climate action into a people-centered development obligation. A climate project should not only ask whether emissions are reduced or resilience is improved. It must also ask whether vulnerable communities are protected, whether workers and livelihoods are supported, whether affected groups participate in decisions, whether benefits are fairly shared and whether accountability systems can prove that no one is left behind.

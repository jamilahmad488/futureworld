#012 Nature-Based, Biodiversity and Watershed Resilience

Climate resilience must protect forests, biodiversity, soil, water and watersheds as living infrastructure that reduces risk while supporting communities, livelihoods and ecosystem recovery.

Strategic definition

Nature-based, biodiversity and watershed resilience means using the protection, sustainable management and restoration of natural or modified ecosystems to reduce climate risk and deliver social, ecological and economic benefits. It treats forests, soils, watersheds, wetlands, rangelands, springs, biodiversity and community-managed landscapes as practical climate infrastructure.

This principle combines international nature-based solution standards with proven local practice from Bajaur and the Merged Districts: assisted natural regeneration, site closures, Village Development Committees, community watchers, grazing control, fire prevention, indigenous species recovery, wild olive grafting, water harvesting and long-term stewardship.

Core rule

Nature-based climate action should restore ecosystems and reduce climate risk for communities.

A nature-based intervention is credible only when it protects biodiversity, improves watershed function, reduces hazard exposure or vulnerability, and has local ownership for maintenance and monitoring.

1Why this principle matters

Climate risk is often intensified by degraded landscapes. Deforestation, overgrazing, uncontrolled fire, soil erosion, poor land management and watershed degradation reduce nature's capacity to absorb shocks. When ecosystems weaken, communities face higher runoff, flood risk, drought stress, slope instability, spring decline, biodiversity loss and livelihood vulnerability.

Professional standard: Nature-based solutions should not be presented as simple tree planting. They must be designed as ecosystem restoration, risk reduction, biodiversity protection, community governance and measurable resilience.
Climate valueHealthy ecosystems reduce heat, runoff, erosion, flood peaks, drought stress and fire vulnerability while storing carbon.
Biodiversity valueNative vegetation, habitat connectivity and species recovery strengthen ecosystem stability and adaptive capacity.
Watershed valueForests, soils and slopes regulate water flow, recharge springs, protect streams and reduce sedimentation.
Community valueLocal stewardship creates jobs, protection systems, awareness, ownership and durable maintenance beyond project closure.

2Locally proven practices from Bajaur and Merged Districts

Assisted Natural RegenerationProtect degraded closures so existing rootstock, seed banks and native vegetation recover naturally with low-cost ecological restoration.
Village Development CommitteesUse democratically organized community institutions to agree protection terms, represent stakeholders and coordinate stewardship.
Community watcher systemHire a local Negehban or Chowkidar to watch closures, discourage grazing, fire and illicit cutting, and report violations.
Grazing and fire controlProtect regeneration through controlled grazing, fire awareness, fire lines, community penalties and local monitoring.
Indigenous species recoveryPrioritize locally suitable vegetation such as wild olive, oak, chir pine, phulai, sanatha, mazri, kikar and other native or adaptive species.
Wild olive and agroforestryUse wild olive grafting, indigenous fruit nurseries and agroforestry to connect restoration with livelihoods and local economic value.
Water harvesting and slope careUse check dams, water harvesting, stream-bank stabilization, soil conservation and slope treatment to support watershed recovery.
Community handoverMove from project-led protection to community stewardship after maintenance, awareness and local governance systems are established.

4Alignment with global standards

The Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework sets a global pathway for living in harmony with nature by 2050 and includes goals and targets for ecosystem restoration, conservation, monitoring, finance and transparency. Nature-based climate action should therefore support biodiversity recovery, ecosystem integrity, connectivity and benefits for people.

UNEP explains that working with nature can address food and water security, climate change and sustainable development while providing ecological, economic and social benefits. IPCC AR6 WGII assesses climate impacts on ecosystems, biodiversity, water, food, livelihoods and adaptation options, making it a core evidence source for this principle.

5What not to do

Nature-based action loses credibility when it is reduced to plantation numbers only.

  • Do not count plants without survival and protection data.
  • Do not ignore grazing, fire and illicit cutting pressure.
  • Do not plant unsuitable species where natural regeneration can recover better.
  • Do not separate watershed restoration from community governance.
  • Do not claim resilience without monitoring water, soil, biodiversity and livelihood outcomes.

Final takeaway

Nature-based climate action is not cosmetic greening. It is living infrastructure for climate resilience. When forests, soils, biodiversity, water and watersheds are protected through community stewardship, assisted natural regeneration, local watchers, grazing and fire control, indigenous species recovery and outcome monitoring, restoration becomes a practical pathway for adaptation, mitigation, disaster-risk reduction and sustainable livelihoods.

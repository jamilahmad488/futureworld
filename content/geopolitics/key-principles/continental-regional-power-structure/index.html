Core definition

Why regional structure matters

World politics is global, but power is often organized regionally: around continents, seas, corridors, blocs, rivalries and neighborhood systems.

States do not operate in empty space. Each state belongs to a regional environment shaped by borders, neighbors, trade routes, languages, histories, conflicts, markets, resources and institutions. A country may be a global power, regional power, middle power, resource power, maritime power, landlocked state, small island state or fragile state. Each category creates different opportunities and vulnerabilities.

Continental and regional power analysis helps FutureWorld explain why one continent may be dominated by maritime routes, another by land corridors, another by energy systems, and another by demographic or climate pressures. The United Nations geoscheme, World Bank regional data, IMF economic data and regional organizations such as the EU, AU, ASEAN and GCC provide useful reference points for comparing regions without exaggeration.123

FutureWorld definition: Continental and regional power structure is the pattern of influence created by geography, state capability, regional institutions, economic networks, military balance, resources, demography, law and historical relationships within and across regions.

Classification model

Types of states within regional systems

01

Global powers

States with worldwide economic, military, technological, diplomatic and institutional reach.

02

Regional powers

States that strongly influence their surrounding region through economy, security, geography or diplomacy.

03

Middle powers

States with meaningful diplomatic, economic or military capacity but limited global dominance.

04

Resource powers

States whose influence is shaped by energy, minerals, water, food systems, forests or critical materials.

05

Maritime powers

States whose ports, fleets, sea lanes, islands, canals or naval reach create strategic importance.

06

Fragile states

States where institutional weakness, conflict, debt, climate shocks or social stress reduce capacity.

Measurement

Indicators for regional power analysis

Geography

Location, coastlines, landlocked status, rivers, mountains, chokepoints and corridors.

Economy

GDP, trade, industry, finance, debt, infrastructure and regional market share.

Security

Military spending, alliances, internal stability, borders and conflict exposure.

Institutions

Governance quality, legal systems, regional organizations and administrative capacity.

Resources

Energy, minerals, water, agriculture, forests, fisheries and critical materials.

Society

Population, education, health, urbanization, migration, identity and social cohesion.

FutureWorld method

Applying the G-D-T-L-S rule

StepQuestion for regional analysisExample application
G — GeographyWhat continent, subregion, coastline, land corridor, river basin or border system shapes the issue?Central Asia is shaped by land corridors and energy routes; Southeast Asia by maritime routes.
D — DataWhat regional evidence exists: GDP, population, trade, military expenditure, climate exposure or institutional indicators?Use World Bank, IMF, UN, SIPRI and regional organization data.
T — TheoryWhich lens explains the region: realism, regionalism, dependency, sea power, heartland, constructivism?Europe may require institutional theory; Eurasia may require heartland and corridor logic.
L — LawWhich treaties, borders, maritime claims, regional agreements or institutional rules apply?AU, EU, ASEAN or GCC frameworks may shape state behavior.
S — ScenarioWhat future pathway is possible: integration, fragmentation, rivalry, reform or resilience?A region may deepen cooperation or fragment under debt, conflict and climate shocks.
FutureWorld practice: compare regions by systems, not stereotypes. Every region contains both strengths and vulnerabilities.

Scenario pathways

Four futures of regional order

Regional integration

Trade, infrastructure, law and institutions deepen cooperation and reduce crisis costs.

Competitive regionalism

Regional powers cooperate selectively while competing over security, technology and markets.

Fragmentation

Debt, conflict, climate pressure or political instability weaken regional coordination.

Resilient regionalism

Local capacity, climate adaptation, connectivity and institutions improve recovery from shocks.

Final FutureWorld insight

Regions are the operating theaters of global power

Continents and regions give global power its practical shape. They determine routes, neighbors, markets, vulnerabilities, alliances, institutions and identities. FutureWorld should therefore analyze every global issue through both worldwide and regional lenses.

Core definition

Why maritime geography matters

Maritime geography explains how seas shape national fortune, trade access, naval posture, energy security, data connectivity and international law.

Most global trade moves through maritime routes, and many states depend on ports, canals, straits, shipping lanes, offshore resources and undersea cables for economic and strategic connectivity. Maritime geography therefore turns the sea into more than physical space. It becomes a system of movement, law, commerce, energy, security and infrastructure.

For coastal states, maritime access can create trade opportunity, naval reach and resource rights. For landlocked states, dependence on transit corridors can create vulnerability. For island states, the ocean can be both a protective buffer and an exposure zone. For all states, maritime disruption can affect fuel prices, food supply chains, insurance costs, shipping delays and diplomatic pressure. UNCTAD identifies maritime transport as the backbone of international trade, while the International Maritime Organization provides the global framework for shipping safety, security and environmental regulation.12

FutureWorld definition: Maritime geography is the study of how oceans, seas, maritime zones, ports, canals, straits, chokepoints and undersea networks shape state power, trade, energy, security, law and future risk.

The United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea is the main international legal framework for maritime zones. It defines rules for internal waters, territorial sea, contiguous zone, exclusive economic zone, continental shelf and the high seas. These categories determine what coastal states may regulate, what resources they may claim, and what navigation rights other states retain.3

Strategic corridors

Canals, straits and chokepoints

Strategic maritime corridors are routes where global movement concentrates. Some are narrow straits, some are artificial canals, and some are alternative routes used when disruption occurs. Their importance comes from the volume of trade and energy that depends on them, the difficulty of replacing them, and the political or security risks around them.

01

Strait of Hormuz

A central energy route connecting Persian Gulf producers to global markets. EIA identifies it as one of the world’s most important oil transit chokepoints.4

02

Strait of Malacca

A major passage between the Indian Ocean and Pacific systems, highly relevant to East Asian energy security and trade connectivity.

03

Suez and Bab el-Mandeb

A linked Europe–Red Sea–Indian Ocean route where regional insecurity can force rerouting around Africa.

04

Panama Canal

An Atlantic–Pacific connector where water availability, infrastructure capacity and shipping schedules affect global logistics.

05

Turkish Straits

Connect the Black Sea to the Mediterranean and link food, energy, naval access and treaty-based governance.

06

Cape of Good Hope

An alternative route around Africa when Red Sea or Suez risks rise, but it adds distance, time, fuel cost and emissions.

Maritime assets

The sea is also infrastructure

Ports

Ports connect sea transport with inland logistics, industry, customs, energy terminals and military mobility.

Naval bases

Naval bases support deterrence, patrols, crisis response and protection of sea lanes.

Undersea cables

Submarine cables carry global digital communication and form a critical layer of economic and security infrastructure.

Energy terminals

Oil terminals, LNG facilities and refineries turn coastlines into energy-security nodes.

Offshore resources

Continental shelves and EEZs can contain fisheries, hydrocarbons, minerals and wind-energy potential.

Maritime services

Shipping insurance, ship repair, port finance, customs and maritime law all shape the value of sea access.

The International Cable Protection Committee and ITU have highlighted the importance of submarine cables for global communications and digital connectivity.5

Law and navigation

Maritime order depends on rules

Maritime corridors are not only strategic spaces; they are legal spaces. UNCLOS governs maritime zones and navigational rights, including rules relevant to innocent passage and transit passage through straits used for international navigation.3 The IMO supports international cooperation on shipping safety, security, pollution prevention and facilitation of maritime traffic.2

Legal rules matter because they allow trade, navigation and resource use to be judged through standards rather than force alone. However, legal interpretation can be contested, especially where sovereignty claims, military operations, fishing rights, offshore energy or strategic competition overlap.

Legal discipline: FutureWorld analysis should separate geographic fact, legal right, political claim, military action and commercial consequence.

Scenario pathways

Four maritime futures

Open corridor scenario

Routes remain open through legal restraint, naval communication, port cooperation and commercial risk management.

Rerouting scenario

Security risks or canal constraints push ships to longer alternatives, increasing costs, delays and emissions.

Infrastructure diversification scenario

States build alternative ports, pipelines, rail corridors, energy terminals and cable routes to reduce exposure.

Escalation scenario

A local maritime incident affects markets, alliances, public perception and military posture. This is a risk scenario, not a prediction.

Final FutureWorld insight

Control of movement is a form of power

Maritime geography shows that power is not only based on land, armies or resources. It is also based on movement. States, companies and citizens depend on the ability to move goods, energy, data, ships and services safely across oceans. When movement is interrupted, the effects can spread across prices, politics, military planning, law and public welfare.

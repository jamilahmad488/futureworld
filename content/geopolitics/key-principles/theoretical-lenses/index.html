Core definition

Why theory matters

A theory is a disciplined lens for asking why actors behave the way they do. It does not replace facts; it organizes facts into explanation.

Geopolitical analysis becomes weak when it only lists events. Theories help explain patterns: why states balance against rivals, why institutions sometimes reduce uncertainty, why identity and historical memory shape diplomacy, why geography affects power, why sea lanes matter, why dependency can persist, and why climate shocks can become security risks.

No single theory explains everything. Realism highlights power and insecurity. Liberalism highlights institutions, trade and cooperation. Constructivism highlights identity, norms and meaning. Sea power theory highlights maritime routes and naval capability. Heartland and rimland theories highlight land corridors and strategic geography. Dependency and world-systems theory highlight unequal economic structures. Climate-security theory highlights how environmental stress can interact with conflict, migration, food and water insecurity.

FutureWorld definition: Theoretical lenses are structured ways of interpreting world politics by identifying the drivers of behavior, the role of geography, the meaning of power, the limits of cooperation and the possible pathways of future change.

International relations theories

Core lenses for world politics

01

Realism

Focuses on states, power, national interest, survival, anarchy, deterrence and balance of power. Useful for security competition and military strategy.

02

Liberalism

Focuses on institutions, cooperation, trade, interdependence, democracy, law and international organizations. Useful for alliances and global governance.

03

Constructivism

Focuses on ideas, identity, norms, legitimacy, culture and historical meaning. Useful for understanding diplomacy, narratives and civilizational stress.

04

English School

Studies international society, order, rules, institutions and the balance between power and law.

05

Critical Theory

Questions power structures, inequality, domination, knowledge production and whose interests are served by global arrangements.

06

Postcolonial Theory

Highlights colonial history, identity, unequal knowledge systems and the experience of states and societies shaped by imperial legacies.

Critical and sociological lenses

Power is also social, historical and narrative

Critical and sociological theories remind analysts that geopolitics is not only material. Social identity, legitimacy, nationalism, civilizational memory, class, media, religion, public trust and historical grievance shape policy choices. This is why two states may interpret the same treaty, alliance or border differently. It is also why narratives can escalate conflicts even before military action begins.

Social conflict lens

Examines inequality, elite competition, class interests and distributional struggle inside and between societies.

Structural functionalism

Looks at how institutions maintain order, coordination and stability within a social system.

Symbolic interactionism

Explains how meanings, symbols, identity and communication shape behavior and perception.

Development and geo-economics

Economic structure shapes geopolitical options

Dependency theory and world-systems theory help explain why some states remain locked into unequal positions in trade, finance, technology or resource extraction. World-systems analysis, associated with Immanuel Wallerstein, studies the global economy through core, semi-periphery and periphery relations.4 Geo-economics then shows how states use markets, investment, sanctions, technology controls, debt, currencies and infrastructure as instruments of influence.

FutureWorld caution: use development theories carefully. They can reveal structural inequality, but they should not be used to remove agency from states, institutions or societies.

Climate-security lens

Environmental stress as a systems risk

Climate-security theory studies how climate hazards can interact with water, food, migration, livelihoods, health, infrastructure and conflict risk. It does not mean climate change automatically causes war. Rather, climate stress can act as a risk multiplier when governance is weak, livelihoods are fragile, or conflict already exists. The IPCC describes climate risks as interacting with non-climatic drivers and affecting human and natural systems.5

FutureWorld use: climate security should be analyzed through geography, data, governance, livelihoods, conflict history, adaptation capacity and future scenarios.

How to choose a theory lens

Security questionUse realism, deterrence theory, balance of power and military geography.
Institutional questionUse liberalism, international law, regionalism and institutional theory.
Identity questionUse constructivism, postcolonial theory and sociological lenses.
Route/geography questionUse sea power, heartland, rimland, network geopolitics and maritime geography.
Development questionUse dependency, world-systems, geo-economics and institutional capacity.

FutureWorld method

Applying the G-D-T-L-S rule

StepQuestion for theory-based analysisExample application
G — GeographyWhich physical location, route, region, coast, border, resource or corridor shapes the issue?Use sea power for chokepoints; heartland/rimland for Eurasian land-sea competition.
D — DataWhat evidence supports or challenges the theory: trade flows, military budgets, votes, treaties, maps, climate data?Do not force the theory; test it against evidence.
T — TheoryWhich lens explains the behavior best, and which lens may be missing?A conflict may need realism for security and constructivism for identity narratives.
L — LawWhich legal framework limits or legitimizes the behavior?Even power politics must be assessed against the UN Charter, treaties or humanitarian law.
S — ScenarioWhat future pathway does each theory suggest?Realism may predict balancing; liberalism may predict institutional bargaining; constructivism may predict norm change.
FutureWorld practice: use theory as a lens, not as a slogan. Compare at least two lenses before drawing conclusions.

Scenario pathways

How theories lead to different futures

Realist future

States balance, deter, compete and form alliances because uncertainty remains high.

Liberal institutional future

Rules, trade, organizations and negotiations reduce conflict costs and support cooperation.

Constructivist future

Ideas, identity and norms change how states define interests and legitimacy.

Geo-economic future

Finance, technology controls, energy systems, industrial policy and supply chains become the main fields of competition.

Final FutureWorld insight

Theory turns information into understanding

Theoretical lenses help FutureWorld avoid shallow commentary. They allow a report to explain why events happen, not only what happened. But every theory has limits. The most responsible method is to combine theory with maps, evidence, law and scenario thinking.

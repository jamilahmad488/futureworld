Executive Summary

Modern power is systemic

Systemic power is the ability of a state, bloc or society to convert resources into durable capability. It is not only military strength, GDP or ideology. It is the capacity to coordinate institutions, finance, industry, technology, skills, logistics, legal systems and public trust. A country may possess resources but fail to convert them into development. Another may have limited natural resources but become powerful through education, manufacturing, innovation and institutional discipline.

The World Bank’s Worldwide Governance Indicators show why governance capacity matters for development and state performance.1 The World Economic Forum’s Global Competitiveness work has long emphasized institutions, infrastructure, skills, innovation and market efficiency as pillars of competitiveness.2 UNIDO’s industrial development reporting also highlights the importance of industrial capacity and manufacturing transformation for development.3

FutureWorld assessment: geopolitical power should be measured by conversion capacity: the ability to turn geography, people, resources, technology and capital into security, prosperity and resilience.

Concept

What is systemic power?

Systemic power describes strength that emerges from the whole system, not from one isolated asset. A military without logistics is vulnerable. Technology without institutions remains underused. Capital without public trust can leave the country. Natural resources without value addition create dependency. Education without employment pathways produces frustration.

FutureWorld definition: Systemic power is the organized capacity of a state or society to align technology, institutions, industry, capital, human skill and strategic culture into long-term national capability.

Capability Conversion Engine

The six layers of durable power

Technology

AI, chips, satellites, cyber systems, data infrastructure and research ecosystems.

Institutions

Rule systems, public administration, courts, planning bodies, regulators and trust.

Industrial Scale

Manufacturing depth, logistics, energy supply, ports, standards and skilled labor.

Capital Markets

Investment, savings, credit, sovereign funds, development finance and risk management.

Human Capability

Education, technical skills, health, digital literacy, creativity and leadership.

Strategic Culture

Long-term planning, national purpose, civic discipline, learning systems and crisis memory.

AI and cognition

AI strengthens systems only when humans remain capable

Artificial intelligence can increase productivity, improve research, support public services and enhance strategic analysis. But AI can also weaken human capability if societies use it as a substitute for learning, judgment and institutional discipline. The OECD’s AI principles emphasize human-centered values, transparency, robustness and accountability.4 UNESCO’s AI ethics recommendation highlights the need for human rights, inclusion, education and governance.5

FutureWorld principle: AI is not a shortcut to power. It is an amplifier. It strengthens societies that already invest in education, institutions, infrastructure and critical thinking.

Legal layer

Power must be read with rules

Systemic power should be analyzed together with international law. The UN Charter provides core principles on sovereignty, peaceful settlement and limits on force.6 International economic law, trade rules, investment agreements, climate commitments and humanitarian law also shape what states can legitimately do. Law does not remove power politics, but it provides standards for evaluating conduct.

Scenario pathways

Possible futures of systemic power

Resilient builders

States invest in institutions, education, infrastructure, technology and climate adaptation.

Resource dependency

States rely on raw material exports without building value chains or institutions.

Digital dependency

Societies adopt external technology platforms without domestic skills, data governance or infrastructure.

Institutional renewal

Governments reform public systems, improve trust and convert demographic potential into productive capacity.

FutureWorld insight

Systemic power is conversion power

The decisive question is not what a country possesses, but what it can organize. FutureWorld should therefore assess states through the G-D-T-L-S method: geography, data, theory, law and scenarios. Systemic power becomes visible when maps, statistics, institutions and future risks are studied together.

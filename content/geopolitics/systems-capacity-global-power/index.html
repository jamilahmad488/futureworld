Executive Summary

Capacity decides what power can achieve

Global power is often described through military spending, GDP, alliances and resources. These are important, but they do not explain everything. The missing factor is systems capacity: the ability of a state or society to coordinate institutions, infrastructure, information, finance, public trust and human skill during normal times and crises.

The World Bank’s work on governance indicators, the UN’s Sustainable Development Goals, UNDP’s Human Development Reports and OECD public governance work all point toward a central idea: development and influence depend on the quality of systems, not only the quantity of resources.123

FutureWorld assessment: systems capacity is the bridge between potential and power. It explains why some societies recover from shocks while others weaken under pressure.

Concept

What is systems capacity?

Systems capacity is the practical ability to make different parts of society work together: government, markets, communities, infrastructure, science, law, education and emergency systems. It is visible when a country responds to floods, manages energy shortages, controls disease outbreaks, educates citizens, maintains roads, collects data, protects forests, regulates markets and provides justice.

FutureWorld definition: Systems capacity is the organized ability of a society to convert institutions, infrastructure, knowledge, resources and trust into effective action, resilience and long-term development.

Capacity dimensions

Five foundations

Governance

Public administration, planning, rule implementation, accountability and service delivery.

Infrastructure

Roads, grids, ports, water systems, telecom networks, data centers and logistics.

Knowledge Systems

Education, research, statistics, AI literacy, technical skills and evidence-based policy.

Resilience

Disaster readiness, public health, food systems, climate adaptation and social protection.

Legitimacy

Public trust, legal fairness, inclusion, social cohesion and political confidence.

Coordination

The ability to align ministries, local bodies, communities, private sector and partners.

Law and institutions

Capacity needs rules

Systems capacity depends on legal and institutional foundations. The UN Charter defines the international system of sovereign states.4 The Paris Agreement creates a climate framework requiring national commitments and adaptation planning.5 International humanitarian law shapes state conduct during conflict, while human rights and development norms influence governance expectations.

Scenario pathways

Four futures of capacity

Capacity upgrade

States invest in public service, data, climate adaptation, education and infrastructure.

Capacity stress

Debt, climate shocks, conflict or energy shortages overload weak institutions.

Capacity dependence

External finance and technology help but create dependency if local capability is not built.

Capacity resilience

Communities, institutions and technology combine to reduce vulnerability and support recovery.

FutureWorld insight

Power is only useful when capacity can direct it

Systems capacity is essential for FutureWorld Geopolitics because it makes power analysis practical. It connects national development, climate resilience, public institutions, security and technology. It also helps visitors understand why the same shock affects countries differently.

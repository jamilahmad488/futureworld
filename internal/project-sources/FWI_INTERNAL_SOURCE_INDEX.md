## 2. Standard Source Hierarchy

### Tier 1: Primary and Institutional Sources
Use for factual backbone and major quantitative claims.

- IPCC — climate science, impacts, adaptation, mitigation, scenarios
- UNFCCC — Paris Agreement, COP process, NDCs, transparency, climate governance
- WMO — climate indicators, weather, early warning, state of climate reports
- UNEP — emissions gap, adaptation gap, environment and climate policy
- FAO — agriculture, forestry, food systems, land, mountains, watersheds
- WHO — climate and health, public health impacts
- World Bank — development, poverty, climate finance, damages, country diagnostics
- IMF — macroeconomics, fiscal risk, debt and global financial stability
- IEA — energy security, transition, critical minerals, emissions, clean technology
- UNHCR — displacement, refugees, human security
- UNCTAD — trade, maritime transport, supply chains, development economics
- SIPRI — military expenditure, arms transfers and security trends
- OECD — AI principles, governance, development and policy standards
- UNDRR — disaster risk reduction and Sendai Framework
- CBD — biodiversity and ecosystem governance

### Tier 2: Academic and Peer-Reviewed Sources
Use for research depth, theory and evidence interpretation.

- Peer-reviewed journal articles
- University research centres
- Academic books
- Published systematic reviews and meta-analyses

### Tier 3: Technical and Policy Sources
Use for expert analysis and policy context.

- Reputable think tanks
- Development agencies
- Professional associations
- Technical working papers
- Sectoral institutions

### Tier 4: News and Current Affairs Sources
Use for event timelines and current context only. Avoid using news sources as the sole support for major scientific, economic or policy claims.

### Tier 5: Weak or Non-Core Sources
Use with caution and never as the main source for major claims.

- Blogs
- Opinion columns
- Social media posts
- Unverified websites
- AI-generated summaries

## 3. Mandatory Source Practice

For every future report:

1. collect sources before drafting;
2. prioritize Tier 1 and Tier 2 sources;
3. create an evidence register;
4. verify every major factual claim;
5. cite sources close to the claim;
6. separate evidence, analysis and projection;
7. complete the citation audit;
8. complete the similarity check;
9. complete the review checklist;
10. maintain an update log.

## 4. Internal Instruction for Future AI-Assisted Workflows

Before starting any new FWI report, the AI assistant, editor or contributor should consult:

`internal/report-writing-architecture/FWI_REPORT_WRITING_ARCHITECTURE.md`

No major report should proceed directly from AI-generated draft to publication. The draft must pass through research planning, source collection, claim verification, citation audit, originality check and human review.

## 5. Public-Facing Position

The internal architecture may remain hidden from readers and unlinked from public pages. Public reports may include a short responsible-use or source-integrity note where appropriate, while the detailed evidence register, citation audit and review files remain part of FWI internal backend governance.
